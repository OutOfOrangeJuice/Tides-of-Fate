Tides of Fate is a combat-focused ttrpg utilising a deck of cards instead of dice. Though the order of each draw is determined beforehand, the player characters have access to a *Hand*, allowing them to overrule the trappings of fate when needed. 

Deck – each player gets a small personal deck with which to draw from, alternate to the draw pile that serves as the primary source of randomness. Whenever a player draws a card, he my choose to pick a card from his deck instead of drawing from the pile, to guarantee the value of a draw.

Downtime – can last as much in-game time as game master determines. Downtime shuffles the card pile, restores everyone's deck, and restores health and other resources.

Progression and class system – you start with 2 archetypes of your choice (or 1 specialised archetype of your choice), depending on your race and background. When you level up (happens at important points in the story), you may upgrade an existing archetype or gain a new one of a category you don't yet have. There are 3 categories – Heroic, Magical, and Buccaneer. Heroic focuses on direct combat ability, Magical on spells and utility, and Buccaneer on support and teamwork. Each archetype has three specialisations to choose from.

Turns in combat – on your turn you have a movement action, a versatile action, and two archetype actions. Movement action allows you to move, archetype action allows you to use an archetype ability, and versatile action can be used for generic actions as well as archetype abilities.

Resources – universal: Health and Willpower. Health represents your ability to tolerate combat and willpower represents your will to keep fighting. If your health drops to 0, your willpower is halved and you take a wound (health is restored by an amount depending on level). If your willpower drops to 0, you are defeated and cannot keep fighting. Some archetypes will have abilities that interact with these systems.

### Game Setup
Centre:
- A draw deck
- A discard deck
The draw deck and discard deck are used for the primary card-based randomness of the game. When a card is used for something, it goes to the discard deck (which is face up)
When the draw deck is used up, flip the discard deck and use it as the new draw deck.

Players:
- A character sheet
- A hand of cards
Each player should have a character sheet that describes the character and allows tracking of abilities and resources, as well as a hand of cards. When a card is used from a hand, it goes to the discard pile.

Other objects such as environments, maps, character minis, a DM screen, etc. are not necessary to play the game but can be used to enhance the experience.

### How Sessions Work
Sessions can be run pretty freely, but the intended balance is to have:
- Start with downtime
- Build up plot with in-combat and out of combat scenes
- Shorter downtime before climax
- Climactic battle
This system benefits more from planned pacing (like a serialised story) rather than a sandbox to do whatever the players want, but it can still be played that way.

### Measurements
Measurements will be done using 'units'. A unit can represent a variable space depending on who is playing and the situation at hand, but generally an average sized character takes up one 'unit' of space and can reach one 'unit' in every direction around him.

### Movement
On a character's turn, he may use his movement action to move a number of units equal to his movement stat. Note that the units of movement per turn is a resource that regenerates at the start of the character's turn – some abilities may spend unused movement.

Characters can actively use one 'type' of movement at once. Depending on race, background, skills, archetypes, etc. characters may have more than one type of movement (e.g. climbing, hovering, swimming, flying). A movement action can always only be used to make one type of movement – you cannot move 2 units and use the remaining 4 units of movement you have to climb something in one movement action. However, you may switch movement types by spending a versatile action to move (Spending a versatile action to move regenerates all movement units). 

When jumping from a standstill, a player character spends three times the movement units per unit of distance jumped. When jumping from a run-up, a player character can jump horizontally a number of units up to a number of units equal to the number of units used as a run-up, but the distance of the jump still requires movement units. Examples:
- A character with 6 units of movement can spend his move action to jump a maximum of 2 units in any direction, spending 3 units of movement per unit of distance jumped.
- A character with 6 units of movement may jump 3 units after making a run-up of 3 units, spending all 6 units. 

Additionally, characters who cannot swim or climb may do so, spending three times the number of units of movement per unit of distance moved.
